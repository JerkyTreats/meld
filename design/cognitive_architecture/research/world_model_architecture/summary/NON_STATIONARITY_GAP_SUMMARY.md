# Non-Stationarity Gap Summary

Source report: [Bayesian Belief Layers Under Non-Stationarity](../pdf/Bayesian%20Belief%20Layers%20Under%20Non-Stationarity.pdf)

Prompt source: [Non-Stationarity and Regime Detection](../../research_prompts.md#5-non-stationarity-and-regime-detection)

## Definitions

- **Non-stationarity:** A condition where the data-generating process changes over time, so old evidence/outcome relationships no longer describe the current regime.
- **Stationary prior:** A prior calibrated under the assumption that future observations come from the same statistical relationship as past observations.
- **Regime:** A latent structural state of the world, such as calm versus crisis, where likelihoods, correlations, transition dynamics, or measurement relationships differ.
- **Regime posterior:** The belief system's posterior probability over which regime is active.
- **Changepoint:** A time when the data-generating process switches from one regime or segment to another.
- **Run length:** In Bayesian online changepoint detection, the time since the most recent changepoint.
- **Continuation model:** The hypothesis that new evidence is still generated by the current regime and should update the active prior.
- **Break model:** The hypothesis that new evidence is better explained by a new regime or fresh segment.
- **Bayes factor:** The likelihood ratio comparing how well two models explain the same evidence.
- **Posterior odds:** Prior odds multiplied by the Bayes factor. This is the relevant comparison for deciding continuation versus break.
- **Posterior expected loss:** The expected cost of each action under the posterior. Break decisions should be thresholded by operational costs, not only by symmetric probability.
- **Regime-entry prior:** A fresh prior used when a new segment begins, instead of continuing to borrow statistics from the prior active regime.
- **Regime library:** An archive of prior regime posteriors that can be reused if a regime recurs.
- **Mixture prediction:** A prediction that averages over possible active regimes weighted by their posterior probabilities.
- **Predictive surprise:** An observation being unlikely under the current predictive distribution.
- **Structural surprise:** Evidence that is better explained by a regime change than by ordinary variation, outliers, or model noise.
- **Belief storm:** A systems-level term for high-churn belief propagation under bursts, conflicts, or adversarial evidence. It is related to but distinct from non-stationarity.
- **Stress test:** A structured, severe, internally coherent counterfactual scenario used to reveal vulnerabilities that ordinary historical calibration may miss.

## Prompt Gap

The prompt targeted this gap:

> The prior calibration loop assumes stationary statistical relationships between evidence and outcomes. In the macro risk management context that motivates this project, regime changes break those relationships structurally, invalidating learned priors.

The research question was how a Bayesian belief layer should decide when surprising evidence should update an existing prior versus trigger a new regime, and how that differs from high evidence churn or adversarial belief storms.

## Answer To The Gap

The report answers the gap by reframing non-stationarity as model uncertainty, not merely strong evidence against the current prior.

The formal choice is not "update versus erase." It is a comparison between a continuation model and a changepoint or regime model. A break is justified when the evidence is better explained by a structural-break model than by continued updating under the old regime, with the action chosen by posterior expected loss.

For Meld, the operational answer is:

- Do not erase history when a break is suspected.
- Maintain posterior probability over continuation, changepoint, and regime identity.
- Start a new active local segment from a regime-entry prior when break probability crosses a cost-calibrated threshold.
- Preserve old regime posteriors in a regime library for recurrence, audit, and smoothing.
- Produce mixture predictions while regime uncertainty remains unresolved.

## Prompt Questions Mapped To Report Answers

### When To Discard A Prior

The report says a prior should not be discarded because a residual is large. It should be set aside for local prediction only when a break model beats the continuation model under posterior expected loss.

Answer: use posterior odds or posterior changepoint probability, calibrated by false-reset versus missed-break costs. Under symmetric 0-1 loss, a break threshold of `0.5` is natural; under asymmetric costs, the threshold changes.

### BOCPD And BCP For Online Detection

Bayesian Online Changepoint Detection is the natural online detector because it maintains a posterior over run length. Posterior mass near run length zero indicates that the newest observation fits a fresh segment better than the continuation of the current one.

Answer: use BOCPD-style online filtering for active detection. Use BCP-style offline/product-partition methods for retrospective segmentation, calibration, and relabeling over replay windows.

### Markov-Switching Priors

Markov-switching models introduce a latent regime variable with probabilistic transitions. Forecasts are mixtures over regimes while identity is uncertain, not hard switches unless posterior mass is near-degenerate.

Answer: maintain both separate regime-specific priors and a mixture model. Separate priors support recurring regimes; mixture prediction handles uncertainty during transitions.

### Stress Testing Beliefs

Supervisory stress testing treats scenarios as severe coherent counterfactuals, not forecasts. The important translation to a knowledge graph is joint extremity: multiple evidence channels failing or moving together.

Answer: adversarial belief evaluation should test correlated graph perturbations, not only single-edge perturbations. Useful outputs include posterior flips, threshold crossings, posterior concentration under attack, recovery time, cascade depth, and blast radius.

### Non-Stationarity Versus Belief Storms

The report distinguishes structural regime change from inference-layer churn. Non-stationarity means the generating mechanism changed. A belief storm means many beliefs revise rapidly because evidence arrives in bursts, conflicts, or propagates through connected structure.

Answer: diagnose them separately. High changepoint probability across consecutive observations points to non-stationarity. Low changepoint probability with high sensitivity to local perturbations points to a belief storm. Both can occur together.

## Design Consequences

This report turns non-stationarity into a concrete belief-layer architecture:

- Add a global structural layer that tracks continuation, changepoint, and regime identity.
- Add a regime layer with archived regime-specific priors, posteriors, and transition probabilities.
- Keep local comparator sufficient statistics scoped to the active regime or segment.
- Reset only the active local segment after a break, not the full history.
- Preserve old regimes for recurrence, audit, and retrospective smoothing.
- Use mixture predictions while the active regime is uncertain.
- Add cost-calibrated thresholds for false resets versus missed breaks.
- Treat isolated anomalies as outlier candidates unless they are structurally better explained by a break model.
- Use retrospective smoothing over replay windows to merge, split, or relabel suspected breaks.
- Add adversarial stress scenarios that perturb several graph evidence channels together.

## Suggested Belief Fields

The report implies the comparator and calibration layer should eventually expose:

- `regime_id`: the current hard-assigned regime, if one is sufficiently probable
- `regime_posterior`: posterior probabilities over candidate regimes
- `changepoint_probability`: probability that the current observation began a new segment
- `run_length_posterior`: distribution over time since the most recent changepoint
- `continuation_score`: evidence for continuing under the active regime
- `break_score`: evidence for starting a new segment or regime
- `posterior_expected_loss`: decision cost used to trigger reset, retention, or mixture prediction
- `regime_entry_prior`: prior used for the new active segment
- `mixture_prediction`: prediction averaged over candidate regimes
- `stress_response_metrics`: posterior flips, threshold crossings, recovery time, cascade depth, and blast radius

## What Remains Open

The report leaves several design choices unresolved:

- Break thresholds need operational cost calibration, not only a fixed probability.
- Gradual drift may need discounting, dynamic model averaging, or time-varying transition probabilities rather than hard changepoints.
- Robust changepoint detection is needed so outliers do not create false pseudo-regimes.
- "Belief storm" is not a standard technical term in the cited literature; it remains a Meld systems concept that should be formalized locally.
- Stress-testing concepts transfer well, but the mapping from financial supervisory scenarios to graph belief evaluation is an architectural analogy rather than a direct prescription.

The practical conclusion is architectural: make regime uncertainty first-class, preserve old priors instead of deleting them, and distinguish structural change from high-churn evidence propagation.
